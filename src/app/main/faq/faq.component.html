Let's bust some myths ...

Let's face facts: we don't live in the tropics. But the good news is that the water temperature can reach a toasy 20°C in the summer months - and this is the sort of temperature that some can tolerate without a wetsuit. However, away from the summer, sea temperatures can get much colder and you will probably need a wetsuit to protect you from the elements if you want to be a year-round snorkeller. The amount of neoprene you need tends to depend on your own physiology and how long you intend to stay in the water.

We have been diving, snorkelling and swimming around Britain for 30 years, and the emphatic response is YES! British waters are teeming with marine life. We have large mammals like seals and dolphins, large fish like sunfish and wrasse, along with a wealth of smaller critters (our favourite is the Tompot Blenny!) You do have to look a bit harder than on a tropic reef, but that game of hide-and-seek is part of the enjoyment. It doesn't take long before you learn where to find your favourites and the reward is enormous!

Snorkelling, like any outdoor activity, comes with risks - that's part of the reason for doing it. But there are many common-sense things we can do to enjoy the marine enviornment safely, but number one on our list will always be: never snorkel alone. It's so important that you have someone to look out for you, even if they stay on the shore. Be aware of your knowledge and experience level and seek advice from locals/experts as necessary.

Britain's coastline is rich and diverse, and you can find marine life almost anywhere. The most abundent locations though are those with rocky seabed with healthy currents: enough to bring nutrients but not too strong that fauna cannot hold on. Rocky areas are a great place to find marine life as it provides a perfect habitat for all kinds of critters - especially when those rocks are covered with seawed (often kelp). Anywhere you can see rocks or kelp from the surface is likely to be a good snorkel site, just make sure you check out the tides and currents before you pop in...
